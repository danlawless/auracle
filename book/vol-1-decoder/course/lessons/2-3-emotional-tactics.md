### Script

In our last lesson, we covered tactics that attack your perception—your ability to know what's true. Today, we're covering tactics that hijack your emotional state and exploit your need for connection.

These are the tactics that make you feel things that benefit the manipulator.

**EMOTIONAL TACTICS**

**Tactic 9: Love Bombing**

Intense, overwhelming attention and affection at the start of a relationship. You're made to feel like the most special person in the world.

They text constantly. They want to see you all the time. They talk about the future, about how you're different from everyone else, about how they've never felt this way.

It feels incredible. It's also a tactic.

Love bombing creates rapid attachment. It floods your brain with dopamine and oxytocin. It bonds you before you've had time to assess who this person actually is.

**The decoder:** Fast intimacy is a red flag, not a green light. Genuine connection builds over time. If it feels too good to be true at the start, slow down.

**Tactic 10: Guilt Induction**

Making you feel responsible for their emotions, their circumstances, their choices.

"After everything I've done for you..."
"You're the reason I'm so stressed."
"If you really loved me, you would..."
"How could you do this to me?"

Guilt is a powerful emotion. When activated, it makes you want to fix things, to make amends, to prove you're not a bad person.

The manipulator harvests that energy.

**The decoder:** Guilt is appropriate when you've genuinely caused harm. Ask yourself: What specifically did I do wrong? If you can't find a clear answer, the guilt is likely manufactured.

**Tactic 11: Shame Induction**

While guilt says "I did something bad," shame says "I am bad."

Shame tactics attack your identity:
"What's wrong with you?"
"No one else would put up with you."
"You're so pathetic."
"You'll never find anyone else."

Shame makes you smaller. It makes you grateful for whatever scraps of approval you receive. It keeps you in relationships you should have left long ago.

**The decoder:** Shame that comes consistently from one source is induced shame. Healthy relationships build you up; they don't systematically diminish you.

**Tactic 12: Emotional Flooding**

Overwhelming you with emotional intensity so you can't think clearly.

Sudden rage. Dramatic tears. Crisis after crisis that demands your immediate attention. Escalating intensity until you capitulate just to restore calm.

When you're flooded, your prefrontal cortex goes offline. You react rather than respond. You agree to things you wouldn't agree to if you could think clearly.

**The decoder:** Notice if they bring up important topics during high emotional intensity. That timing isn't an accident. Important decisions should wait until calm.

**Tactic 13: Manufactured Crisis**

Creating emergencies that demand your attention and prevent you from addressing real issues.

Every time you try to have an important conversation, something happens. A health scare. A work crisis. A family emergency.

The crisis diverts your attention and generates sympathy that makes your concerns feel petty.

**The decoder:** Track the pattern. If crises consistently appear when you're about to address problems in the relationship, the timing is not coincidental.

**RELATIONAL TACTICS**

These tactics exploit your connection needs—your attachment, your loyalty, your desire for closeness.

**Tactic 14: Intermittent Reinforcement**

This is the most powerful bonding mechanism known to psychology.

Sometimes they're wonderful. Sometimes they're cruel. You never know which version you'll get.

This unpredictability creates intense attachment. Like a slot machine, the variable reward schedule keeps you pulling the lever. The occasional jackpot of affection feels more valuable because it's inconsistent.

**The decoder:** Consistency is a sign of health. If you're constantly trying to figure out what mood they're in, if you walk on eggshells, if good moments feel like rewards you've earned—you're being trained by intermittent reinforcement.

**Tactic 15: Triangulation**

Introducing a third party to create jealousy, competition, or validation.

"My ex never complained like you do."
"Everyone else agrees with me."
"This other person really appreciates me."

Sometimes the triangle is an actual person—someone they flirt with, confide in, or compare you to. Sometimes it's imaginary—the vague "everyone" who always agrees with them.

The purpose is to make you feel like you need to compete, to be grateful, to try harder.

**The decoder:** Healthy relationships don't require you to compete for attention. If you consistently feel like you're being compared, you're being triangulated.

**Tactic 16: Silent Treatment**

Withdrawal of attention as punishment.

Not a healthy request for space—that would include communication: "I need some time to process this. I'll reach out tomorrow."

Silent treatment is different. It's unexplained withdrawal designed to make you panic, chase, and eventually apologize—even if you did nothing wrong.

**The decoder:** There's a difference between needing space and weaponizing silence. The first includes communication and timeline. The second leaves you confused and anxious.

**Tactic 17: Hot and Cold**

Alternating between extreme closeness and sudden distance.

One day they're completely available, loving, present. The next, they're unreachable, cold, dismissive. No explanation.

This keeps you off-balance and focused on them. You spend your energy trying to figure out what changed, what you did wrong, how to get the warm version back.

**The decoder:** If you're constantly analyzing their mood and adjusting your behavior to manage it, you're living in their emotional weather. That's not partnership—it's subordination.

**Tactic 18: Future Faking**

Making promises about the future that never materialize.

"Once we move in together, things will be different."
"I'm going to change—you'll see."
"Next year, we'll take that trip."
"When things calm down, I'll have more time for you."

The future is always better. But the future never arrives. The promises keep you invested while delivering nothing.

**The decoder:** Judge relationships by current behavior, not future promises. If you've been waiting for things to get better for more than a few months, they're not going to.

**Tactic 19: Obligation Creation**

Doing things for you that you didn't ask for, then using those things as leverage.

"I paid for dinner, so you owe me."
"I helped you with that thing, and this is how you repay me?"
"After everything I've sacrificed for you..."

The gifts weren't free. They were investments in future control.

**The decoder:** Real generosity comes without strings. If favors are regularly invoked as debt, you're not being helped—you're being positioned.

**The combined effect**

When emotional and relational tactics work together, you end up in a state of anxious attachment.

You're afraid to upset them (guilt and shame).
You're bonded through chaos (intermittent reinforcement).
You feel like you owe them (obligation).
You're waiting for the future they promised (future faking).
You feel less-than compared to others (triangulation).

This is not love. This is conditioning.

## Key Concepts

- Emotional tactics hijack your feelings
- Relational tactics exploit your need for connection
- Love bombing creates fast, unstable attachment
- Intermittent reinforcement is the most powerful bonding mechanism
- Future faking keeps you invested while delivering nothing
- Combined effect: anxious attachment that feels like love but is conditioning
