### 1.2 Parenthetical Research Notes and Disclaimers

**Current state - Various formats observed:**

**Format A - Parenthetical note in body text (Chapter 2, lines 56-58):**
> "Dr. Stephen Porges, the neuroscientist who developed Polyvagal Theory, coined the term "neuroception" to describe the process by which our nervous system evaluates risk without our conscious awareness. (Note: While Polyvagal Theory has been influential in trauma therapy, some aspects remain contested within neuroscience. The core insight about unconscious threat detection, however, is well-supported by research.)"

**Format B - Parenthetical note in body text (Chapter 4, lines 100-101):**
> "Many trauma recovery practitioners recommend a minimum of 72 hours, though this is a practical guideline rather than a scientifically validated timeframe."

**Format C - Parenthetical note in body text (Chapter 4, line 274):**
> "(Note: The term "codependency" has been criticized by some clinicians for pathologizing what are often adaptive trauma responses. We use it here because it remains widely recognized, while acknowledging that these patterns often developed as survival strategies in unsafe environments.)"

**Format D - Parenthetical note in body text (Chapter 4, lines 362-364):**
> "These are general patterns drawn from clinical experience—your individual timeline may vary significantly based on relationship length, trauma history, support systems, and other factors."

**QUESTIONS FOR AUTHOR:**
1. Do you want a consistent format for research disclaimers/caveats?
2. Should parenthetical research notes use "(Note: ...)" or be integrated into the sentence without the "Note:" label?
3. Should longer research caveats (like the Polyvagal Theory note) be formatted as footnotes or remain in-text?
4. Do you want to standardize the em-dash style (e.g., "your individual timeline may vary—based on...") versus parenthetical style for these asides?

**Recommended decision:** Choose one consistent format for all research caveats and disclaimers.
