### Claim 2.3: Cognitive Dissonance

**Location:** Chapter 4, lines 88-104

**Claim:** Cognitive dissonance occurs when someone alternates between charm and cruelty, resolved by minimizing the bad, maximizing the good, blaming self, and hoping for change.

**Verification Status:** ⚠️ DISCREPANCY FOUND

**Findings:**
The manuscript conflates **cognitive dissonance** (Festinger's theory) with **trauma bonding** dynamics. While related, they are distinct:

**What cognitive dissonance actually is:**
- Uncomfortable feeling when holding contradictory beliefs
- Resolved by changing beliefs, adding beliefs, or reducing importance
- Developed by Leon Festinger in the 1950s

**What the manuscript describes:**
- Trauma bonding, which USES cognitive dissonance as one mechanism among several
- Also includes intermittent reinforcement, power imbalance, isolation

**Recommendation:**
Either rename the section to "Trauma Bonding" and note that cognitive dissonance is one component, OR more accurately describe Festinger's theory before applying it.

## Part 3: Quotes and Attribution

### Claim 3.1: Gloria Steinem Quote

**Location:** Chapter 1, lines 444-445

**Quote:** "The truth will set you free, but first it will piss you off." — Gloria Steinem

**Verification Status:** ⚠️ DISCREPANCY FOUND

**Findings:**
Gloria Steinem popularized this quote and uses it frequently, but she did **not originate it**:
- The saying was in circulation by 1990, before Steinem's use
- Earliest matches appear in 12-step recovery culture
- Steinem began using it in speeches by 1998
- She published a book with this title in 2021

**Steinem's own words:** She attributes it to the anti-draft movement and feminist consciousness-raising, not claiming it as her original creation.

**Recommendation for Correction:**
Change attribution to one of:
- "The truth will set you free, but first it will piss you off." — commonly attributed to Gloria Steinem
- "The truth will set you free, but first it will piss you off." — origin disputed; popularized by Gloria Steinem
- Add footnote explaining the quote's evolution

**Sources:**
- Quote Investigator: The Truth Will Set You Free, But First It Will Piss You Off
- Steinem, G. (2021). *The Truth Will Set You Free, But First It Will Piss You Off!*

## Part 5: Historical and Cultural References

### Claim 5.1: Wolf in Sheep's Clothing

**Location:** Chapter 1, lines 59-81

**Claim:** "An ancient fable" about a wolf wrapping itself in wool.

**Verification Status:** ⚠️ DISCREPANCY FOUND

**Findings:**
- The **phrase** originates from the Bible (Matthew 7:15, Sermon on the Mount)
- The **fable** with a wolf disguising in sheep's skin dates to the **12th century CE** (Nikephoros Basilakis)
- This is 1,500+ years after Aesop lived (620–564 BCE)
- Modern attribution to Aesop is incorrect

**Recommendation:**
Change "ancient fable" to either:
- "a fable famously attributed to Aesop" (acknowledges common attribution)
- "a medieval fable" (more accurate)
- Remove the historical claim entirely and just use the metaphor

### Claim 5.2: Frog in Boiling Water

**Location:** Chapter 1, lines 138-169

**Claim:** "If you drop a frog into hot water, it jumps out immediately. But if you place it in cool water and slowly raise the temperature, it stays until it boils."

**Verification Status:** ⚠️ DISCREPANCY FOUND (Scientifically Inaccurate)

**Findings:**
This is a **scientifically debunked myth**:
- Harvard biologist (1995): "If you put a frog in boiling water, it won't jump out. It will die."
- University of Oklahoma herpetologist (2002): "The legend is entirely incorrect!" Frogs become more active and attempt escape as water heats.

**Important:** The metaphor is widely used (particularly in organizational behavior and climate discussions), but scientists note it should only be used **if clearly labeled as a metaphor, not biological fact**.

**Recommendation:**
Change the framing to explicitly identify it as a metaphor:
- "There's a well-known metaphor—even though the biology isn't accurate—about a frog in water..."
- "This popular analogy, though scientifically dubious, captures something true about..."
- Simply state "This is a metaphor for..." rather than presenting it as if frogs actually behave this way
